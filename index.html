finding jobs suck

...even after college...

but why?

There's loads of reasons. Competition being one of them. but let's go over the other possible reasons:
  • entry-level jobs are unpaid internships in scarcity.
  • nobody has "20 years of experience" out of college.
  • schools might actually not be preparing you well.
  • your dad isn't working at Goldman Sachs.
  • you're trying to benefit from a crumbling system.

terminology

skills-based hiring

degrees suddenly stop mattering. you can get hired now because you have cool projects to show off.

gpa inflation

colleges are trending towards raising the mean GPA of their students, which questions the value of a degree. even worse, this is happening at ivy leagues as well.

cultural capital

refers to symbols or ideas that can be used as resources in social action. for our sakes, it's degrees and internships.

youth talent programs

basically the reemergence of child labor, but it's okay because kids might find it fun and it helps with the future.

crumbling?

It's because of the internet.
universities were propped up because information was sacred and unbelievably hard to find. but now, some really prestigious topics, crafts and fields are being converted to trades that can be trained on the internet.

convincing graph

credentials

prestige

 georgiana mihut ran an experiment with sending different types of template resumes to employers and found that applications in the high skills match condition were 79% more likely to receive a callback than applications in the low skills match condition and that college prestige was not statistically significant (mihut). this demonstrates that college prestige is trending towards obsolescence, with having the actual skills for the job being much more important than where one graduated from. if one is able to build those skills independently, then it shows how much university credentials are starting to matter less over time.

inflation

 not only is inflation prevalent, disfiguring the credibility of gpa and grades, but because it’s more likely in weather schools means that either wealthier students are treated in higher regards due to their gpa or lower-income students are burdened with the stigmas of gpa inflation which they aren’t affected by. in either case, the lower-income students lose. this class disparity demonstrates the unfairness and crumbling of our education system, which indeed, might discourage newer employers from looking at gpa altogether. the decay of meaningful credentials of education may seem grim, what choice are lower-class “students” left with if education isn’t even reliable enough to get a job?

merit

 degree requirements completely being thrown out seems extreme, but as evident in previous studies, a reality with accredation programs being more valuable than degrees is already slowly being realized. furthermore, if the future entails non-degree certification programs to grant degrees, then class mobility will see a revolution as the money and time needed for a university degree is no longer needed to prove you actually know or have a skill. in this future, everyone with an ability to do something will be able to do something with that ability thereby basing class only on one’s ability.
